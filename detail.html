5 Recruitment tips for your community program

Hello, my name is Emmanuel Misiati. My life changed for the better in late 2019 because of only one decision – deciding to participate in a phenomenal program that I think you and everyone else should try. In a nutshell, this program saved me from a depressive and lonely period, gave me a community, literally handed me my self-belief, and opened me up to my current career path. To say that this program is transformational is an understatement. You just have to experience it yourself.

If you are a facilitator of any community program you might find yourself doing a version of this pitch when recruiting your next cohort’s participants. Having an overwhelming number of people expressing willingness to be part of your cause is a problem you are willing to entertain as compared to the reverse. Convincing enough people to be part of your program is not an evening stroll in this age of overpouring distraction, insatiable personal and professional needs, and ‘unavoidable busyness’. As such, employing some techniques and paying attention to useful insights will save you a lot of trouble in your recruitment process.

Here are a few key lessons I have picked up over the years while recruiting young people for the Taifa Teule Leadership Experience program. (TLX)

1.Know your target audience and where to find them

Aimless recruiting is akin to shooting in the dark. Missing your mark is inevitable. Our communities are organized not only for functionality but also for easier identification. Understanding who you want to join your program is the first step to knowing where and how to find them. Being a youthful program, I learned that prospective TLX participants can be found in churches, academic institutions, cyber cafes & gaming places not forgetting local chill spots (base). Knowing where I need to go saved me time, resources, and character development where shooting my shot seemed unproductive.

2.Put a face to the program/recruitment

Who would you trust more? A ghost trader or one you have met or seen? One on one recruitment offers a higher success rate as compared to colorful posters and catchy captions. Sparing time to visit the local chief, church or institute of learning inspires confidence in the key persons that you interact with and who will later assist you in recruitment. Where graphics lack in detail and elaboration, in-person communication gives you the opportunity to present the best case for your program yourself. What about social media / online recruitment? Show your face! Borrowing a leaf from influencers, I learn that sometimes people relate to the personality they are seeing. Do not be afraid to turn on that camera and share with your audience the goodness that is in store for them.

3.Always lead with Value

Naturally, human beings are constantly doing a cost-benefit analysis of their time and resources. However flowery your invitation pitch may be, your prospect’s mind is already wondering ‘what is in it for me?’ It is only natural. Therefore, punctuate your invitation with benefits that participants of your program will enjoy. Share personal inspiring stories! Graduation and certificates aside, what more can an individual get from heeding your call? People relate to other people’s experiences. In my three years of recruitment for TLX, I have found myself retelling how the experience changed my life first before even outlining what the program entails. This captures people’s attention for the rest of the ‘boring’ never changing aspects of the recruitment pitch. Your experiences communicate the best value.

4.Have a short and simple registration process.

No one wants to spend years signing up for anything. Having a two or three step sign up process ensures that your prospect will follow through with their decision to join your program. Usually, we simply request TLX prospects to share their number and location of residence only. From this, a call up list is generated through which further communication is made.

5.Have a short and simple registration process.

It’s either a YES or a NO. Often, we hesitate to ask the main question to prospects due to the fear of rejection. The risk to this is having a recruitment process that tappers off into uncertainty. Finish off with a yes or no question and express the importance of that clarity to the next stage of your program. This greatly helps you in knowing for sure how many participants will be joining you and how many declined your invitation. Where possible, ask the latter (the ones who said NO) for their reasons and see if something needed to be done differently. Remember, not asking is an ABSOLUTE loss. What could possibly go wrong? Just ask

Apart from the above, I have also learned that recruiting is similar to doing sales. You have to understand your product and your customer. You have to practice your pitch and know the ‘hot’ words and phrases that they will endear to. And you are the first impression of what you are selling. Looking and sounding the part is not a bonus but a building block of your recruitment process. Lastly, and interestingly, people are ready, willing, and hungry to be part of something great – something beyond themselves. They just need you to show them what you do and hold their hand into joining your circle.

I wish you well as you start, continue or end your recruitment process. I hope this helps and that you also reach your target. What other recruitment tips do you know of that are helpful? Kindly share with us in the comment section.

Emmanuel Marumbu Misiati

Founder & CEO Afro Beta Consult
